# Illumios Academia Session 1 Facilitator Notes

Last updated: 2026-04-14  
Session title: `Find Your First AI Wins`  
Length: 90 minutes

## Session Goal

By the end of this session, each participant should leave with:

- a clearer understanding of what AI is useful for in a small business
- a list of real tasks they do repeatedly
- 3 prioritized AI use cases worth testing in their own business
- homework that prepares them for Session 2

## Teaching Standard

This session should feel practical, calm, and low-hype.

You are not trying to impress people with AI.
You are helping them recognize where they are already losing time and where AI could help first.

## Materials Needed

- participant worksheet
- your own copy of the worksheet with an example business filled in
- one AI chat tool ready for a quick demo
- optional timer for exercises
- optional slides with only a few anchor points

## Tone To Hold

Keep repeating versions of these lines:

- "We are looking for useful, not flashy."
- "Your best first AI use case is usually boring."
- "You do not need to automate your entire business."
- "We are aiming for one clear win, not ten half-starts."

## Run of Show

### 0:00-0:10 Welcome and Frame the Program

#### Objective

Lower anxiety and set the right expectations.

#### What To Say

"This program is designed to help you get one real result with AI in your business. We are not trying to turn you into a technical expert. We are trying to help you identify where AI can save time, reduce busywork, and support better workflows in the real work you already do."

"Today is about finding your best opportunities. By the end of the session, you should know your top 3 AI use cases and which one is the best place to start."

#### Questions To Ask

- What kind of business do you run?
- What made you interested in this program?
- What is one repetitive task you would love to spend less time on?

Keep introductions brief. Do not let the room drift into long business backstories.

### 0:10-0:20 Teach What AI Is Good At

#### Objective

Give participants a simple mental model for what AI is actually good at.

#### Whiteboard or Slide Points

AI is useful for:

- drafting
- summarizing
- rewriting
- organizing
- turning rough input into cleaner output

AI is not good at:

- making final business decisions for you
- handling sensitive communication without review
- replacing your judgment
- understanding your business context unless you provide it

#### What To Say

"A lot of people get stuck because they ask, 'What can AI do?' That question is too broad. A better question is, 'What repeated work in my business involves drafting, summarizing, organizing, or rewriting?'"

"If a task is repeated, annoying, and easy to review, it is usually a good candidate."

### 0:20-0:30 Teach Where Small Businesses Lose Time

#### Objective

Help them connect AI to business friction, not abstract capabilities.

#### Common Friction Areas To Name

- follow-up emails
- proposal or estimate drafts
- meeting recaps
- intake summaries
- FAQ responses
- internal process notes
- content repurposing
- lead follow-up

#### What To Say

"Most small businesses do not need a futuristic AI strategy. They need relief from recurring work that drags on every week."

"The best first use cases are often the tasks you delay because they take too long, the tasks you redo from scratch, or the tasks that always sit in your head longer than they should."

### 0:30-0:50 Exercise 1: Task Inventory and Friction Audit

#### Objective

Get participants out of theory and into their actual workflow.

#### Instructions

Direct participants to the worksheet and say:

"I want you to write down the work you actually do, not the work you wish your business looked like."

"Start with three buckets:

1. 5 tasks you repeat every week
2. 3 tasks you dread
3. 2 tasks you procrastinate because they take too long"

"Do not overthink it. We are trying to surface reality."

Give them 8 to 10 minutes to fill the first section silently.

Then ask them to mark each task with quick notes:

- how often it happens
- how frustrating it is
- how easy it would be to review if AI helped draft it

#### Coaching Prompts While They Work

- "What do you rewrite from scratch more often than you should?"
- "Where do you keep saying, 'I know I need to respond to that'?"
- "What task eats time but does not really require original thinking every time?"

#### Debrief Questions

- Which tasks showed up in more than one list?
- Which tasks happen often and still feel annoying?
- Which tasks already follow a pattern, even if you have never written the pattern down?

### 0:50-1:00 Teach the Prioritization Method

#### Objective

Help participants choose smart starting points instead of shiny ones.

#### Framework

Score each task from 1 to 5 on:

- Frequency
- Friction
- Risk
- Value

Explain `Risk` carefully:

- low-risk means AI can help draft or organize, and a human can easily review before it goes out
- high-risk means errors would matter a lot, or the task involves sensitive judgment

#### What To Say

"For your first AI win, we are not looking for the most ambitious task. We are looking for the task with the best mix of repetition, frustration, business value, and low downside."

"If you can review it quickly before using it, that is usually a good sign."

### 1:00-1:20 Exercise 2: Choose Top 3 Use Cases

#### Objective

Turn the task inventory into a ranked list of starting opportunities.

#### Instructions

Have participants choose 5 candidate tasks from their worksheet.

Ask them to score each one, then sort them.

Then say:

"Circle your top 3. Put a star next to the one you believe is the best first win for the next two weeks."

After most people finish, ask them to complete this sentence for each top use case:

"AI could help me with this by..."

Examples:

- drafting a first version
- summarizing messy information
- turning rough notes into a clean response
- organizing repeated information into a reusable format

#### Facilitator Support

If someone picks a bad first use case, steer gently.

Common bad picks:

- something too strategic
- something too technical
- something they rarely do
- something too risky to test comfortably

Use language like:

"That may become a good later use case, but for this program I would start with something more repeated and easier to review."

### 1:20-1:30 Share-Outs, Reflection, and Homework

#### Objective

End with momentum and a clear bridge to Session 2.

#### Share-Out Prompt

Ask each participant to share:

- their number-one use case
- why they chose it
- what they think AI could help them do in that task

Keep each share to 60 seconds.

#### Homework Instructions

"Before Session 2, bring one real example of your top use case. That could be a draft email you need to send, meeting notes, a proposal, intake notes, a customer question, or some rough content."

"Bring the messy real thing, not a polished version. Session 2 is where we turn that into useful prompts."

#### Closing Script

"If today worked, you should leave feeling less overwhelmed, not more. You do not need ten AI ideas. You need a short list and one smart starting point."

## Suggested Demo Examples

Use only one or two demos. Do not over-demo.

### Demo Option 1: Follow-Up Email

Show:

- a rough situation summary
- a weak email draft
- how AI can help turn it into a clearer first draft

Lesson:
AI can reduce blank-page drafting time.

### Demo Option 2: Meeting Notes to Action Items

Show:

- messy notes
- AI turning them into a short summary with next steps

Lesson:
AI is good at organizing and summarizing.

### Demo Option 3: FAQ Answer Draft

Show:

- a repeated customer question
- AI drafting a cleaner answer

Lesson:
AI can help standardize common communication.

## Common Participant Reactions and How To Handle Them

### "I do too many different things."

Say:
"That is normal. We are not solving everything today. We are looking for repeated work that can be made easier."

### "I am not technical."

Say:
"Good. This program is designed for business owners, not technical teams."

### "What if AI gets it wrong?"

Say:
"That is why we start with low-risk tasks and keep human review in the loop."

### "I already tried AI and it was bad."

Say:
"That usually means the task was unclear, the prompt was vague, or the use case was not a great fit yet. Session 2 is where we fix that."

## Facilitator Checklist

Before the session:

- fill out the worksheet for one example business
- choose one demo example
- decide what tool you will demo with
- print or send the worksheet ahead of time

During the session:

- keep the room moving
- protect exercise time
- redirect abstract questions back to real tasks
- keep reminding participants that one useful win is enough

After the session:

- send a recap
- remind them about homework
- note the most common use cases participants picked
- use those examples in Session 2

## Success Signal

Session 1 succeeded if most participants can clearly say:

- "Here are my 3 best AI opportunities."
- "Here is the one I should start with."
- "I know what real example I am bringing to the next class."
